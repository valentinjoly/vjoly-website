Domesticated 6,000–10,000 years ago in the Andean highlands of southern Peru {% cite Spooner-2005-ID5025 Spooner-2014-ID5048 %}, the cultivated potato (Solanum tuberosum L.) has quickly become one of the major staple foods worldwide {% cite Birch-2012-ID5043 %}. With a global production of 377 million tonnes in 2016, for a total gross value of $111 billion, the potato is now the fourth most important food crop in the world, after rice, wheat and corn {% cite FAO-2016-ID5061 %}.

Current potato breeding efforts aim to improve yields, food processing qualities, resistance to abiotic stress (e.g. drought), and resistance to pathogens {% cite Halterman-2015-ID5034 Hameed-2018-ID5033 %}. In their search for traits of interest to be introgressed into S. tuberosum, breeders can rely on the large botanical diversity of potatoes encompassing 4 cultivated and 107 wild species {% cite Spooner-2014-ID5048 %}, distributed throughout Latin America (Figure 1), from New Mexico to Patagonia {% cite Hijmans-2001-ID24 Hijmans-2002-ID1260 %}.

Being adapted to a broad array of environmental and climatic conditions, wild potatoes possess desirable agricultural traits, such as higher disease and drought resistance. Recent genomic studies in the potato clade (Solanum sect. Petota) indeed revealed the extraordinary diversity of wild potato germplasm available for potato improvement {% cite Hardigan-2017-ID4307 Li-2018-ID5049 %}.

S. chacoense: a resource for potato breeding…

My project deals with the reproductive biology of Solanum chacoense (Figure 2), a wild potato species whose genome was recently sequenced {% cite Leisner-2018-ID5050 %}. In breeding research, S. chacoense is studied for its high capacity to resist to a wide range of pathogens, such as potato virus Y {% cite Bravo-Almonacid-2012-ID5041 Duan-2012-ID5037 %}, bacteria causing potato wilt {% cite Bae-2008-ID5044 Chen-2013-ID5039 %} and common scab {% cite Braun-2017-ID5042 Jansky-2017-ID5032 %}, or fungi responsible for potato black scurf and blackleg {% cite Almasia-2008-ID5046 %}, and powdery mildew {% cite Faccio-2011-ID5052 %}.

Solanum chacoense has also a peculiar secondary metabolism producing high levels of specific glycoalkaloid compounds, such as leptines and leptinines {% cite Mweetwa-2012-ID5028 %}, that are particulary efficient against the Colorado potato beetle {% cite Lorenzen-2001-ID5053 %}. This small herbivore insect (Figure 3) is a major potato pest that can completely destroy cultures by feeding on leaves. Effort is being made to better understand genes involved in those metabolic pathways {% cite Manrique-Carpintero-2013-ID5030 Manrique-Carpintero-2014-ID5029 %} and to introduce them into the cultivated potato {% cite Cooper-2009-ID5055 Ginzberg-2012-ID5036 Molnar-2017-ID5054 Crossley-2018-ID5038 %}.

…and a model for plant reproduction research

Being a diploid species, S. chacoense is also studied as an alternative model to the tetraploid cultivated potato for molecular and cellular biology, especially in plant reproduction research. For instance, in our research institute, S. chacoense has been used in the Cappadocia lab to decipher the mechanisms underlying S-RNase-based gametophytic self-incompatibility {% cite Boivin-2014-ID3411 Soulard-2014-ID1031 Soulard-2013-ID5026 Liu-2012-ID38 %}. The Geitmann lab, now at McGill University, used S. chacoense in their research about cytomechanics of pollen tube growth {% cite Aouar-2010-ID5045 Parre-2005-ID3995 Parre-2005-ID5027 %}.

The Matton lab uncovered various proteins involved in reproductive signal transduction pathways in S. chacoense, including RALF peptides {% cite Chevalier-2013-ID866 Germain-2005-ID4437 %}, MAP kinases {% cite Daigle-2015-ID4432 Lafleur-2015-ID2009 O'Brien-2007-ID4452 Gray-Mitsumune-2006-ID4263 %} and receptor-like kinases {% cite Germain-2013-ID142 Germain-2008-ID1587 Germain-2007-ID854 Germain-2005-ID1215 %}. Our lab also produced large-scale transcriptomic {% cite Tebbji-2010-ID4182 %}, proteomic {% cite Vyetrogon-2007-ID4931 %} and secretomic {% cite Liu-2015-ID2691 %} studies about S. chacoense reproduction.

My PhD project aims at understanding how pollen–pistil interactions, especially pollen tube guidance, are involved in the reproductive isolation of wild potatoes, i.e. the mechanisms by which they avoid interspecific hybridization.
