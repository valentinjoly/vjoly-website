Wild potatoes (Solanum sect. Petota) form a large taxon gathering 196 species distributed in Latin America, from New Mexico to Patagonia {% cite Hijmans2001 Hijmans2002 %}. My PhD project aims at understanding their reproductive isolation, i.e. the mechanisms by which they avoid interspecific hybridization.

I am especially interested in Solanum chacoense, a wild potato relative of agronomical importance, and three of its closest relatives in the phylogeny: S. gandarillasii, S. tarijense, and S. microdontum. Before going further on my project, let me say a few words about these species.

A model for plant reproduction research

Being a diploid species, S. chacoense is also studied as an alternative model to potato for molecular and cellular biology, especially in plant reproduction research. For instance, in our institute, S. chacoense has been used in the Cappadocia lab to decipher the mechanisms underlying S-RNase-based gametophytic self-incompatibility {% cite Boivin2014 Soulard2014 Soulard2013 Liu2012 %}. The Geitmann lab, now at McGill University, used S. chacoense in their research about cytomechanics of pollen tube growth {% cite Aouar2010 Parre2005 Parre2005b %}.

The Matton lab uncovered various proteins involved in reproductive signal transduction pathways in S. chacoense, including RALF peptides {% cite Chevalier2013 Germain2005b %}, MAP kinases {% cite Daigle2015 Lafleur2015 OBrien2007 GrayMitsumune2006 %} and receptor-like kinases {% cite Germain2013 Germain2008 Germain2007 Germain2005 %}. Our lab also produced large-scale transcriptomic {% cite Tebbji2010 %}, proteomic {% cite Vyetrogon2007 %} and secretomic {% cite Liu2015 %} studies about S. chacoense reproduction.
